For this week's entry, I wanted to learn about embedding videos in HTML.

YouTube seems to really encourage embedding videos in other sites. When you click the share button below a video, the first option is to embed. It is even listed before copying the link or sharing to a different social media platform. This makes the process incredibly easy as YouTube will provide a premade code sample: 

        
            <iframe width="560" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/9YffrCViTVk" title="YouTube video player" 
            frameborder="0" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture" 
            allowfullscreen></iframe>

I still had one question, though. Is this totally legal? Can anybody rip anyone else's YouTube video and put it on their website without asking? A quick Google search led to this write-up. Under the Berne Convention all original artistic works are copyrighted the moment they are created whether the owner asserts it or not. If you want to use a picture, video, music clip, etc. on your public site 100% legally, perfect practice would have you obtain a license from the copyright holder. Of course, this is rarely the way the internet actually operates. The unwritten rule is: if you don't want people embedding your videos, turn off the aforementioned embed link. Since you can turn off or on the ability to embed a video, it is implied that leaving it on means you are fine with others embedding the vid.

For tech-savvy content creators (like the tutorial channel I used above), this should be fine. But, not everyone knows about the embed toggle or what it does. The article mentions a Reddit post of someone getting sued for embedding a video on their site. Even though the video creator let the video be embedded, they were seeking hundreds of thousands from a random blogger who did just that. At the time of writing, there was no verdict as to whether the case would be thrown out or not. I dug around a bit and found the post.

They were being offered a $170k settlement but threatened for $700k if this deal wasn't taken. Most of the internet-lawyers seemed to think there was no case and that the poster was within their rights. Unfortunately, the poster never left an update as to what happened. But I think the lack of follow-up probably indicates everything worked out. Either way, it's a bit scary that something as innocent as displaying a YouTube video on your website could have you contacting lawyers or worse.
